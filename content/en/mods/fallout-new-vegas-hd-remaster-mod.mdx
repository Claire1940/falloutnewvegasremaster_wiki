<Section icon={Mountain} title="World Textures: Remastering the Mojave">

The core of any visual overhaul is the environment. The Mojave Wasteland is vast, and the original textures are often muddy. To achieve a remaster-level look, you need a layered approach to texture replacement.

**Texture Pack Comparison**

| Pack Name | Focus Area | Recommended Version |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **NMC's Texture Pack** | Roads, buildings, and large structures | Large or Medium (2K) |
| **Ojo Bueno** | Clutter, vending machines, and vehicles | High (2K) |
| **Char's FMV HD** | Landscape and specific architecture | Landscape Only |
| **Landscape Texture Improvements** | Ground seams and transition textures | Standard |

For the best results, install **NMC's Texture Pack** first. It covers roughly 90% of the game's world. However, some of its landscape textures can feel "off" compared to the original art style. By overwriting the landscape files with **Char's FMV HD**, you maintain the vanilla color palette while gaining incredible detail.

<Steps>
  <Step num="1" title="Base Layer Installation">
    Install **NMC's Texture Pack (Large or Medium)**. This provides the foundation for all buildings and roads.
  </Step>
  <Step num="2" title="Clutter and Objects">
    Overlay **Ojo Bueno**. This mod excels at small details like the Nuka-Cola machines and wooden crates that NMC might miss.
  </Step>
  <Step num="3" title="Landscape Refinement">
    Install the landscape files from **Char's FMV HD**. This fixes the blurry ground textures seen in the distance.
  </Step>
  <Step num="4" title="LOD Generation">
    Use **Vanilla Generated LOD** to ensure that buildings and terrain don't "pop in" as you walk toward them.
  </Step>
</Steps>

<Callout type="info" title="Technical Note">
While 4K texture packs exist, the New Vegas engine often struggles with them, leading to "Purple Texture" bugs where the engine runs out of video memory. For 2026 hardware, **2K textures** provide the perfect balance of visual fidelity and stability.
</Callout>

</Section>

<Section icon={Sun} title="Lighting and Atmosphere: Beyond the Yellow Filter">

One of the most defining (and controversial) aspects of New Vegas is its yellow tint. While it adds to the "heat" of the desert, it often crushes the color depth of the remastered textures we've just installed.

To modernize the atmosphere, we recommend a script-based lighting approach rather than a heavy ENB. ENBs often break shadows and cause transparency issues with water and steam.

| Lighting Mod | Primary Effect | Atmosphere Impact |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Yellow Goodbye** | Removes the aggressive yellow color grading | Clean, natural daylight |
| **General Lighting Overhaul** | Brightens days and darkens nights realistically | High immersion |
| **Atmospheric Lighting Tweaks** | Fixes interior image spaces and templates | Better interior depth |
| **Lumen Ambient Lighting** | Makes light-emitting objects actually cast light | Realistic night scenes |
| **Afterglow** | Adds ambient glow to neon signs in Vegas | Essential for the Strip |

<Callout type="success" title="Visual Breakthrough">
By combining **General Lighting Overhaul** with **Lumen**, you create a world where light feels physical. Walking into the Prospector Saloon at night will feel significantly more atmospheric as the fireplace actually illuminates the room and the NPCs within it.
</Callout>

Don't forget the sky. **Cloud Upgrade NVSE** adds pseudo-animated clouds that move across the horizon, while **Heat Haze** emulates the shimmering effect of desert heat on distant objects. These small details sell the "Remaster" feeling more than high-res rocks ever could.

</Section>
